## Overview

Different user groups adopt new products at different times, forming a bell-shaped curve. The real danger isn't the curve itself—it's the invisible chasm between early adopters and early majority.

**Creator:** Everett Rogers

**Year:** 1962

---

## Five Adopter Segments

| Segment | Percentage | Description |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| **Innovators** | 2.5% | Tech enthusiasts |
| **Early Adopters** | 13.5% | Visionaries |
| **Early Majority** | 34% | Pragmatists |
| **Late Majority** | 34% | Skeptics |
| **Laggards** | 16% | Traditionalists |

---

## Key Insight (Geoffrey Moore 1991)

Early adopters are "visionaries" who buy revolutionary change prospects; early majority are "pragmatists" who buy verified productivity tools. These two groups want fundamentally different things.

---

## Case Study: Salesforce Crossing the Chasm (1999-2004)

Focused on medium-sized financial services companies as beachhead market. Shifted messaging from "Kill Software!" to "Your competitors already use this."

**Results:** 4 years of deliberate strategy, $1.1B IPO market cap (2004), $26B revenue (2023)

# Innovation Diffusion Curve (Rogers)

Design segment-specific strategies to move adoption through Innovators, Early Adopters, Early Majority, Late Majority, and Laggards.

## Steps

1. Identify which adopter segment your product currently serves and where the adoption frontier sits.
2. Assess readiness to cross to the next segment — especially the **chasm** between Early Adopters and Early Majority.
3. Tailor messaging: Innovators (technology), Early Adopters (vision), Early Majority (pragmatic proof), Late Majority (safety), Laggards (necessity).
4. Select appropriate channels and social proof for each target segment.
5. Define crossing triggers, reference customer targets, and whole-product gaps to close.

## Output Format

Current segment diagnosis, chasm risk assessment, segment-specific messaging and channel plan, and milestone triggers for each transition.
